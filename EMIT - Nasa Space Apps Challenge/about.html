About

When calcite or chlorite mineral rock dust is stirred up by powerful winds on one continent, the airborne particles can travel hundreds of miles and have an impact on completely separate continents. The atmosphere and the surface of the Earth can be heated or cooled by dust hanging in the air. The Earth Surface Mineral Dust Source Investigation (EMIT) mission of NASA aims to better understand this heating or cooling impact.


Other consequences of mineral dust on our planet are numerous. It may alter atmospheric chemistry or aid in the formation of clouds. The dust can supply nutrients for ecosystem growth when it settles in bodies of water or on land. Mineral dust can increase solar absorption and hasten melting if it falls on snow or ice. Airborne mineral dust can impair human health or diminish visibility.

Scientists are aware that the majority of the mineral dust that travels through Earth's atmosphere originates from arid or dry regions of the world. However, they are unsure of the specific minerals that the wind brings back from those areas. distinct minerals have distinct effects on the environment. Therefore, in order to better understand how the dust is influencing the Earth, scientists need to be aware of what minerals are present in dust source regions. This missing dust source information will be supplied by EMIT. The information will enable researchers to produce a fresh mineral map of the areas of Earth that produce dust. The map will help scientists' computer simulations better predict how mineral dust may affect regional and global warming and cooling in the present and the future.


Dark signal, calibration, and validation targets as well as EMIT coverage mask for arid and surrounding terrain. Each orbit includes additional areas that are collected to account for timing ambiguity.

EMIT was developed at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory and launched on July 14, 2022. The instrument will observe Earth from outside the International Space Station. Once it begins operation, EMIT data will be delivered to the NASA Land Processes Distributed Active Archive Center (DAAC) for use by other researchers and the public. Robert O. Green is principal investigator of EMIT and a senior research scientist at the NASA Jet Propulsion Laboratory.
