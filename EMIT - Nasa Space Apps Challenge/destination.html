Destination

The International Space Station (ISS), one of the most prestigious scientific facilities in the solar system, will serve as the EMIT instrument's operational base. Over 20 years of continuous human presence in space have been supported by the ISS. The pressurised modules where astronauts live and work are the setting for up to twenty laboratory experiments at once. EMIT will also ride along with the ISS, which can accommodate up to 20 experiments at once outside the station. The EMIT team launched the instrument on a SpaceX resupply mission on July 14, 2022. Once mounted on the ExPRESS Logistics Carrier 1 (ELC1) — one of the station's four primary hubs for externally-mounted instruments — EMIT will begin to map the surface composition of Earth's arid land mineral dust source regions. From there, the instrument will study our planet's mineral dust source regions for 12 months.

EMIT is planned to launch on a SpaceX supply mission to the International Space Station (ISS). Once mounted on the ExPRESS Logistics Carrier 1 (ELC1), EMIT will begin to map the surface composition of the Earth’s arid land mineral dust source regions.

EMIT is the only instrument flown on any spacecraft to date that is specifically designed to examine this phase of the Earth's mineral dust cycle. It will be the first experiment on the station to observe a mineral dust source. The equipment will orbit the Earth approximately every 90 minutes when mounted on the ISS, moving at a speed of 5 miles per second (8 kilometres per second), or 16 orbits each day. EMIT was taken off of the SpaceX Dragon spacecraft and put on the International Space Station using the 55-foot, seven-jointed Canadarm-2. Engineers and scientists on Earth will run the cutting-edge gadget.
